## 2.2 Climate, Hydrology, and Groundwater Reliance

Karaga District experiences a semi-arid tropical climate characterised by a pronounced single wet season and an extended dry season. Rainfall is concentrated between May and October, with a marked decline in precipitation outside this window. Mean annual rainfall across northern Ghana is generally lower than in the south, and inter-annual variability is considerable <sup>27,28</sup>. The dry season, which spans approximately six months, results in the cessation of surface flow in many seasonal streams and watercourses, leaving groundwater as the only practically accessible water source for rural communities during that period.

Recharge to the Voltaian aquifer system is episodic and largely controlled by infiltration during the wet season <sup>29</sup>. The semi-arid character of the climate means that evapotranspiration rates are high relative to precipitation, which limits the proportion of rainfall that translates into effective recharge and can produce concentration effects in shallow groundwater as the dry season progresses. These concentration dynamics are particularly relevant for dissolved constituents such as fluoride, sodium, and bicarbonate, whose activity in the hydrochemical system is partly amplified by reduced dilution outside the rainy period. Groundwater is therefore not only the year-round water source in the district but also a chemically evolving resource whose quality varies with the seasonal recharge cycle and with the hydrogeological properties of the host formation <sup>26</sup>.
