## 2.3 Geology and Hydrogeological Background

Karaga District lies entirely within the Voltaian sedimentary basin, one of the principal sedimentary sequences in West Africa and a hydrogeologically significant formation in northern Ghana. As illustrated in Figure 1, the mapped geology of the district is strongly asymmetric in its lithological distribution. The Bimbila Formation underlies most of the northern, central, western, and much of the eastern parts of the district, making it the dominant host unit for the groundwater sampled in this study. The undifferentiated Obosum Group is confined mainly to the southern to south-western margin, while the Bunya Formation occurs discontinuously, most prominently as an east-central to south-eastern belt and as smaller isolated outliers toward the north-west. Fault traces cut across this Voltaian sedimentary succession and provide structural pathways that may influence groundwater flow, mixing, and chemical evolution <sup>26</sup>.

The hydrogeological context of these formations is one of intergranular and fracture-controlled aquifer systems typical of sedimentary sequences in Sub-Saharan Africa <sup>30</sup>. Groundwater occurs in weathered and fractured zones within the sedimentary units, and the depth, yield, and chemistry of individual boreholes are influenced by the local lithological character, degree of fracturing, and proximity to fault structures. The predominantly siliciclastic composition of the Voltaian sediments promotes silicate weathering as a major driver of groundwater chemistry, and the contrast between the Bimbila Formation and the Obosum and Bunya formations introduces lithostratigraphic variability that is expected to manifest in measurable hydrochemical differences across the sampling network <sup>26</sup>. Fluoride occurrence in this setting is understood to be geogenic, deriving from weathering of fluoride-bearing minerals within the sedimentary pile, consistent with the broader pattern of fluoride enrichment documented across Voltaian and adjacent sedimentary groundwater systems in northern Ghana <sup>5,26,31–33</sup>.
