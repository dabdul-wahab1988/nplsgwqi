## 2.4 Land Use and Likely Anthropogenic Pressures

Land use in Karaga District is dominated by subsistence and smallholder agriculture, with food-crop production, livestock grazing, and dry-season vegetable farming constituting the principal economic activities. Settlement activities including open defecation, improper solid-waste disposal, and the proximity of pit latrines to borehole infrastructure can introduce anthropogenic nitrogen into shallow groundwater, providing a plausible pathway for nitrate enrichment in the sampled waters. However, the absence of heavy industry, large-scale irrigation schemes, or significant point-source discharges means that industrial contamination is unlikely to be a major contributor to the observed hydrochemical character of groundwater in the district.

Anthropogenic pressure in Karaga District is therefore best understood as a secondary modifier superimposed on a geogenic hydrochemical template that is principally shaped by rock-water interaction, ion exchange, and the semi-arid climatic concentration mechanism. Localised nitrate enrichment attributable to sanitary and agricultural practices may elevate dissolved nitrogen at specific sites, but the broader hydrochemical character of the groundwater, including fluoride enrichment, bicarbonate-dominated facies, and ion exchange signatures, is predominantly geological in origin <sup>26</sup>. This framing is consistent with published findings from comparable northern Ghanaian and Voltaian settings, where geogenic processes have been identified as the primary driver of major-ion and fluoride chemistry, with anthropogenic inputs acting as a spatially variable secondary signal <sup>5,7,26,34</sup>.
