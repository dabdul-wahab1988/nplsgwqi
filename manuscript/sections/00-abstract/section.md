# Abstract

Groundwater in semi-arid sedimentary basins serves as a critical resource, yet its quality is often shaped by complex and competing geochemical drivers. This study evaluates groundwater quality and health risks within the Voltaian sedimentary basin of northern Ghana. An integrated compositional data analysis and uncertainty-aware framework was applied to 34 dry-season groundwater samples. Results indicate that groundwater is predominantly of the Na-HCO<sub>3</sub> type, governed largely by silicate weathering and forward ion exchange. A neutrosophic groundwater quality index classified most samples as excellent, though a subset proved unsuitable due to intense mineralisation. Bayesian driver profiling revealed that fluoride enrichment is inherently multicausal, driven by competing salinity increase (evapoconcentration/mineralisation) and ion-exchange suppression. Probabilistic health risk assessments identified children as the most vulnerable group, showing a 69% probability of exceeding the skeletal-dental hazard threshold under the modelled dry-season exposure scenario. However, these probabilistic estimates are constrained by the single-season nature of the sampling and represent a temporal snapshot rather than a definitive annual baseline. Ultimately, this approach successfully uncovers the competing geochemical mechanisms governing water quality, offering a transferable evaluation tool for analogous sedimentary environments.

**Keywords:** Compositional data analysis; Neutrosophic logic; Fluoride contamination; Irrigation suitability; Bayesian regression
