## 5.7 Groundwater Management Implications

The findings of this study carry several practical implications for groundwater management in Karaga District and in comparable rural settings within the Voltaian sedimentary basin. These implications are organised around four priority areas: targeted borehole intervention, systematic monitoring, risk communication, and irrigation advisory use.

Borehole screening and targeted intervention are indicated where fluoride-related health risk is the dominant concern. The four samples classified as unsuitable by NPLS-GWQI and the subset of sites where deterministic $HQ_{F}$ exceeds unity or where Bayesian $P\left( HI_{skeletaldental} > 1 \right)$ is elevated should be prioritised for technical remediation, source substitution, or defluoridation before being used for domestic consumption. Because fluoride enrichment in Karaga District is geogenic and distributed in relation to the Bimbila Formation geology and associated geochemical processes rather than to a single point source, blanket treatment of all boreholes is unlikely to be cost-effective. The spatial structure of the NPLS-GWQI classification and the N-ISI outputs therefore provide a practical basis for tiered management, directing scarce intervention resources to the subset of sites where risk is demonstrated rather than assumed.

Benchmarking against conventional WQI baselines shows that broad ranking agreement does not guarantee identical categorical decisions, particularly in the degraded tail where class compression and weight uncertainty can alter boundary classifications (Table 22–Table 23; Supplementary Figure S11). For operational screening, this implies that marginal and degraded sites should be prioritised for confirmatory sampling and follow-up rather than treated as deterministically classified, while sites consistently flagged as unsuitable across multiple formulations warrant immediate intervention.

Monitoring and sanitary protection are especially warranted at sites where nitrate concentrations approach guideline thresholds, signalling the presence of anthropogenic nitrogen inputs from agricultural or sanitary sources. Although nitrate hazard quotients remain below unity on average across all demographic groups in the current dataset (Table 10), the single-season snapshot character of the sampling campaign means that nitrate levels at specific sites could be higher during or immediately after the wet season when flushing of surface-derived nitrogen into shallow aquifers is most active. Regular monitoring at suspected high-nitrate locations, combined with inspection of borehole sanitary protection and exclusion zones, would provide early warning of deteriorating conditions.

Risk communication to communities and local health authorities should prioritise children as the most vulnerable demographic, consistent with the Bayesian posterior finding that $P\left( HI_{skeletaldental} > 1 \right) = 0.6875$ for children under the modelled exposure scenario (Table 11; Figure 9). This vulnerability is a function of physiological exposure differences, lower body weight and higher relative ingestion rate, rather than of unusual behavioural patterns, meaning that it applies broadly to the child population in any community served by groundwater with elevated fluoride concentrations <sup>7,8</sup>. Health communication should convey the distinction between drinking-water quality class, which may appear acceptable for the majority of adults, and the child-specific fluoride risk, which can remain elevated even when the generalised quality index is not in an alarming category.

For irrigation, the N-ISI classification provides an actionable advisory framework. The five excellent and three good samples (Table 13; Figure 10) are suitable for direct agricultural use without restriction. The 23 marginal samples should be used with agronomic caution, including crop-selection for salt-tolerant varieties, soil monitoring for sodium accumulation, and periodic leaching to prevent long-term permeability degradation. The three unsuitable samples should be excluded from irrigation unless blended with better-quality water. The EC-SSP and EC-SAR proxy plots (Figure 10a and 10b) offer field-accessible visualisation tools for district agricultural extension officers to communicate site-specific irrigation risk without requiring detailed chemical knowledge.
