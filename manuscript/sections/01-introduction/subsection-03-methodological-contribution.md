## 1.3 Methodological contribution

The literature documents an established pattern of integrated groundwater assessment studies that combine hydrochemical facies diagnosis with drinking-water indexing, health-risk evaluation, and irrigation suitability assessment in a sequential pipeline <sup>18–21</sup>. However, these frameworks are typically stacked rather than structurally coupled. Process discovery is conducted in conventional hydrochemical space using Piper diagrams, raw-ion correlations, and standard multivariate methods. Downstream risk and suitability modelling then proceeds without formally importing the compositional structure identified in the preceding step. Reviews of groundwater quality assessment and groundwater pollution analytics consistently identify the absence of a single architecture linking compositional process discovery to uncertainty-aware decision modelling as an outstanding methodological gap <sup>22–24</sup>. The practical consequence of this uncoupled approach is that downstream management decisions are often based on distorted driver profiles. For example, applying conventional regression or Euclidean indexing to raw closed-sum major ions can produce spurious correlations where a harmless ion appears as a primary pollution driver simply due to the dilution of a genuine contaminant. If this flawed compositional structure is fed blindly into a health-risk or suitability model, regulators risk misdirecting limited remediation resources toward the wrong hydrochemical target. A further fragmentation is evident within the advanced-method literature itself: ILR-based compositional data analysis, fuzzy and neutrosophic water quality indexing, Bayesian driver modelling, and probabilistic health-risk assessment have each been developed in largely separate research streams, with few studies integrating all four into one reproducible workflow <sup>12,17,24,25</sup>.

The present study addresses this gap through a dual-space compositional process framework applied to 34 groundwater samples from Karaga District, Ghana. While applying such an advanced statistical architecture to a restricted dataset presents inherent risks of overfitting, the selected methods are structurally designed to mitigate small-sample volatility. Specifically, bootstrapped neutrosophic variable importance stabilizes weight estimation across resamples, robust PCA guards against the disproportionate leverage of multivariate outliers, and the Bayesian horseshoe prior is mathematically optimized to extract sparse, informative signals even when the predictor block is large relative to the sample size. In process space, ILR-based compositional principal component analysis with parallel analysis eigenvalue retention and deterministic geochemical auditing is used to identify latent hydrochemical signatures without closure bias. In assessment space, a neutrosophic partial least squares groundwater quality index (NPLS-GWQI) with bootstrapped variable-importance-in-projection weighting is used to integrate water quality information. Bayesian horseshoe regression supports driver profiling for both NPLS-GWQI and fluoride. A hierarchical Bayesian multi-pathway risk assessment (HBMPRA) is applied to fluoride and nitrate across three demographic groups, while entropy-weighted neutrosophic irrigation suitability indexing (N-ISI) evaluates agricultural suitability. Together, these components provide an uncertainty-aware and demographically disaggregated decision layer.
