## 1.2 Limits of conventional assessment approaches

Conventional arithmetic groundwater quality indices represent a widely adopted screening tool yet carry systematic methodological limitations that become consequential when management decisions rely on them. Their construction typically fixes weights, thresholds, and sub-index functions without accounting for threshold ambiguity, measurement uncertainty, or variable reliability, so the final quality class can be sensitive to arbitrary design choices and can imply more certainty than the underlying data justify <sup>9,10</sup>. Monte Carlo uncertainty analysis of conventional water quality index structures demonstrates that weight uncertainty alone is large enough to alter class membership, while Bayesian model-averaging results confirm that multi-model combination materially improves reliability over any single deterministic index <sup>10,11</sup>. When threshold ambiguity and variable reliability are left unaddressed, arithmetic indices can mask which variables drive impairment, create false precision around class boundaries, and collapse structurally uncertain evidence into one overconfident score <sup>9</sup>.

A second methodological problem arises in the multivariate interpretation of hydrochemical data. Groundwater chemistry is compositional: major-ion concentrations are constrained by the sum of dissolved solids and carry relative rather than absolute information. Application of Euclidean-based methods, principal component analysis, hierarchical clustering, or correlation analysis, to untransformed raw-ion data can therefore generate spurious correlations, distorted inter-sample distances, and component loadings that reflect the geometry of closure rather than genuine hydrogeochemical structure <sup>12</sup>. Isometric log-ratio transformation addresses closure by projecting compositions into unconstrained Euclidean space, and recent groundwater studies demonstrate that isometric log-ratio (ILR) transformation-based approaches improve discrimination of facies contrasts and polluted versus background groundwater compared with their raw-space counterparts <sup>12,13</sup>.

Deterministic hazard quotient and hazard index methods constitute a third limitation when applied as the sole characterisation of non-carcinogenic health risk. Point-estimate hazard quotient (HQ) and hazard index (HI) values conceal the variability attributable to population-level differences in body weight and ingestion rate, seasonal fluctuations in contaminant concentration, and pathway-specific exposure uncertainty. Probabilistic approaches such as Monte Carlo simulation and Bayesian hierarchical modelling explicitly propagate uncertainty into full risk distributions and exceedance probabilities. Comparisons between deterministic and probabilistic estimates consistently show that deterministic methods communicate a more certain, and sometimes misleading, risk picture than the available data support <sup>14–17</sup>. However, while probabilistic simulations excel at propagating documented parameter variability, they are not a panacea for data scarcity; they remain fundamentally constrained by the accuracy of the underlying exposure distributions. If local dietary habits, seasonal ingestion variations, or alternative exposure pathways are unmeasured, Bayesian frameworks can inadvertently generate a false sense of security by quantifying uncertainty around an inaccurate baseline.
