## 1.1 Groundwater dependence and water-quality relevance

Groundwater serves as the principal source of domestic water supply across rural northern Ghana, where piped water infrastructure remains limited or unreliable and borehole water is often the sole accessible resource for everyday consumption and household sanitation <sup>1</sup>. In districts such as Karaga, the same groundwater resource also underpins dry-season smallholder irrigation, with local knowledge guiding well siting and seasonal water management in ways that demonstrate a dual domestic–productive dependency rather than a strictly potable function <sup>2</sup>. This dependence acquires immediate public-health significance in the context of the Voltaian sedimentary basin, where national-scale hazard modelling estimates that approximately 15% of Ghana’s land area, concentrated in the northeast, carries elevated fluoride risk, placing an estimated 920,000 people in proximity to potentially unsafe groundwater <sup>3</sup>. Locally, empirical studies in adjacent Voltaian-setting districts confirm that hydrochemistry is heterogeneous even over short distances. In districts such as Karaga, predominantly underlain by the siliciclastic Bimbila Formation, silicate and carbonate mineral weathering act as the primary geogenic controls on major-ion and fluoride evolution (Sunkari et al., 2025, 2026). This rock-water interaction is frequently modified by forward ion exchange and semi-arid concentration effects. Furthermore, the reliance on subsistence agriculture introduces localized anthropogenic pressures, such as potential nitrate enrichment from pit latrines and agricultural runoff, which superimpose a secondary contamination signal onto the natural geogenic template <sup>4–6</sup>. Crucially, navigating this complex dependence demands a framework that does not conflate drinking-water quality, human health risk, and irrigation suitability.

Ghana-based evidence consistently demonstrates that a groundwater source acceptable for smallholder crop production may simultaneously pose a chronic fluoride exposure risk, particularly for children, who face disproportionately higher non-carcinogenic hazard owing to lower body weight and higher relative fluid intake <sup>7,8</sup>. Treating these evaluative dimensions as interchangeable can distort risk characterization. It may overstate safety in some use categories while understating risk in others. This approach can also obscure opportunities for targeted management responses, such as source substitution or protective treatment (Ganyaglo et al., 2019; Araya et al., 2022).
