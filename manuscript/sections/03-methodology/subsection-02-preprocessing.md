## 3.2 Preprocessing and Stoichiometric Conversion

Prior to multivariate analysis, relevant ionic concentrations were converted from mg/L to milliequivalents per litre (meq/L) using valence-based stoichiometric factors. Charge-balance error (CBE) was computed for each sample from the ratio of the difference between summed cationic and anionic equivalents to their total sum, expressed as a percentage. Samples with CBE values within an acceptable analytical tolerance were retained without modification.

The measured variables were partitioned into two functional blocks for downstream modelling. The compositional hydrochemical block, consisting of Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Na⁺, K⁺, HCO₃⁻, Cl⁻, SO₄²⁻, NO₃⁻, and F⁻, was treated as a nine-part composition subject to ILR transformation before any multivariate analysis. The field and non-compositional block, consisting of pH, EC, and TDS, was handled outside the compositional framework and used directly in water-quality indexing and as predictors in the augmented endpoint-driver models. For endpoint-driver modelling, an augmented predictor block was constructed by combining log-transformed ionic concentrations, field variables, and derived geochemical indices and proxies including the chloro-alkaline indices $CAI_{1}$ and $CAI_{2}$, the Na/Cl molar ratio, the Ca/HCO₃ ratio, the Mg/Ca ratio, a silicate weathering proxy, and a carbonate weathering proxy. Nitrate and fluoride served explicitly as health-risk endpoints throughout the assessment space.

**CBE thresholding and use.** For reporting, we adopt ±10% as the primary CBE acceptance threshold commonly used for natural-water major-ion datasets and additionally report the stricter ±5% screen requested by the reviewer. The full CBE distribution (Figure 3) and per-sample values (Table 16) are therefore used as a *data-integrity diagnostic* rather than as a hard exclusion rule. Given the limited sample size (n = 34) and the risk of distorting spatial/geological coverage, no samples were removed solely on the basis of CBE; instead, CBE exceedances are carried forward transparently as a limitation and interpreted alongside the Bayesian and benchmarking robustness checks (Section 3.9; Table 19–Table 23).

**Detection-limit metadata.** Analyte-specific LOD/LOQ values and any censoring flags were not available in the supplied dataset materials. Consequently, concentrations were treated as uncensored point values in ILR-based compositional analysis and in downstream Bayesian models. This limitation is explicitly noted in the interpretation of low-concentration tail behaviour and uncertainty (Sections 3.4, 3.6, and 4.5).
