## 3.5 Deterministic Geochemical Constraints

A suite of deterministic geochemical indices was computed to constrain and corroborate the process interpretations arising from the compositional discovery step. These indices were derived from the meq/L-converted dataset and provided a classical geochemical audit of the hydrochemical system rather than a competing analytical pathway. Gibbs-domain assignment was performed using reconstructed TDS and the ratio of dominant cationic and anionic species, placing each sample within the precipitation-dominance, rock-weathering, or evaporation-concentration fields of the Gibbs diagram to characterise the overall geochemical control mechanism.

Ion-exchange behaviour was quantified through the chloro-alkaline indices $CAI_{1}$ and $CAI_{2}$, computed from the stoichiometric relationship between chloride and the sodium and potassium concentrations, with strongly negative values indicating forward cation exchange in which calcium and magnesium replace sodium on aquifer mineral surfaces, and positive values indicating reverse exchange.

The Na/Cl molar ratio was used as an indicator of halite dissolution relative to silicate weathering, with values substantially above unity indicating that sodium is derived predominantly from mineral weathering and exchange rather than from simple halite dissolution. A silicate weathering index and a carbonate weathering index were additionally computed to characterise the relative contribution of silicate and carbonate mineral dissolution to the groundwater chemistry.

**Speciation and saturation-index support (PHREEQC).** To provide mechanistic support for carbonate buffering and fluoride mobilization hypotheses, saturation indices (SI) were computed using PHREEQC via the `phreeqpython` interface. Solutions were parameterised using the measured major ions, pH, and alkalinity (as HCO₃⁻) under a compatible database (`phreeqc.dat`). Because sample temperatures were not included in the tabulated dataset, SI values were evaluated under a small temperature band (20, 25, and 30 °C) and reported for calcite, dolomite, and fluorite (Table 18; Figure S9). Fluorapatite SI could not be evaluated with the available inputs because phosphate was not measured, and is therefore treated as an explicit limitation in interpretation.
