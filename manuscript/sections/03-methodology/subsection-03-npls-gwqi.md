## 3.3 NPLS-GWQI

Groundwater quality was assessed using a neutrosophic partial least squares groundwater quality index (NPLS-GWQI), a framework which in principle addresses key limitations of conventional arithmetic indices by incorporating threshold uncertainty, variable reliability, and ambiguity in class membership <sup>9,10</sup>. Each measured variable was first normalised against its corresponding WHO or Ghana Standards Authority (GSA) guideline value <sup>35</sup> to produce a parameter-specific sub-index, $q_{i}$. A bipolar hazard treatment was applied to pH rather than a unidirectional upper-threshold penalty, reflecting the fact that both acidic and highly alkaline deviations from neutrality represent quality concerns. Neutrosophic membership was modelled through three independent components, truth ($T$), indeterminacy ($I$), and falsity ($F$), which capture the degree to which a sample definitively meets, ambiguously approaches, or clearly fails the adopted quality standard.

To transition from parameter-level memberships to a singular index, variable importance weights were estimated using the neutrosophic_pls (see github repo at <https://github.com/dabdul-wahab1988/neutrosophic_pls>) computational package <sup>36</sup>. Specifically, weights were derived via bootstrapped neutrosophic variable importance in projection (N-VIP) (see details in supplementary method S1). By recalculating weights, $w_{i}$, across repeated resamples of the neutrosophic dataset, the algorithm extracts dynamic, data-driven variable contributions rather than relying on fixed, expert-assigned weights. Details of the NPLS-GWQI formulation is presented in Supplementary methodology S1. The final NPLS-GWQI score for each sample was computed as the weighted sum of the truth components:

$$
\text{NPLS-GWQI}_{s} = \Pi_{s} \cdot \sum_{i = 1}^{p}\widehat{w_{i}} \cdot \text{SI}_{i,s}
$$

where, $\text{NPLS-GWQI}_{s}$ is the final Neutrosophic Partial Least Squares Groundwater Quality Index score for a specific groundwater sample $s$, $\Pi_{s}$ is the sensory–aesthetic penalty factor applied to sample $s$, $p$ is the total number of hydrochemical parameters included in the index (in this study, $p\  = \ 12$), $i$ is the index representing an individual hydrochemical parameter (e.g., pH, EC, Nitrate), $\widehat{w_{i}}$ is the bootstrap-mean dynamic weight assigned to parameter $i$. These weights are derived using a PLS-based weighting scheme and stabilized via 500 bootstrap resamples to reflect the expected contribution of each parameter, $\text{SI}_{i,s}$ is the per-parameter sub-index for parameter $i$ in sample $s$. It is calculated by multiplying the normalized hazard ratio ($q_{i,s}$) by a neutrosophic scalar amplifier ($\Psi_{i,s}$) and a constant factor of 100. Final NPLS-GWQI scores were classified into five quality classes, Excellent, Good, Poor, Very Poor, and Unsuitable, using the standard classification thresholds adopted for neutrosophic groundwater quality indexing (Supplementary method S1.9).

**Benchmarking against conventional alternatives.** To quantify the practical benefit of NPLS-GWQI relative to established indexing approaches, we benchmarked NPLS-GWQI against (i) a conventional arithmetic WQI computed from the same hazard-ratio matrix under equal weights, and (ii) a fuzzy-only variant (truth-membership only) with the same equal-weight structure. To address the reviewer’s request for Monte Carlo weight uncertainty, the conventional arithmetic WQI was additionally evaluated under Dirichlet-perturbed weights centred on the equal-weight vector, producing per-sample class probabilities and a most-probable class with confidence (Table 23).
