## 4.3 Groundwater-Quality Structure and Classification

Across the worsening quality gradient, truth membership declined and falsity rose progressively, whilst indeterminacy remained comparatively low and was most visible around class-transition boundaries rather than at the quality extremes (Figure 3a). The groundwater-quality structure is strongly right-skewed: 25 of the 34 samples were classified as excellent, 1 as good, 1 as poor, 3 as very poor, and 4 as unsuitable (Figure 3d). The four unsuitable samples form a distinct degraded subset rather than ambiguous edge cases, as reflected in their low indeterminacy values reported in Table 3.

The bootstrapped N-VIP dynamic weights assigned to the contributing variables are presented in Table 4. EC, Cl<sup>-</sup>, TDS, Ca<sup>2+</sup>, K<sup>+</sup>, and Na<sup>+</sup> received the highest weights, demonstrating that mineralisation and salinity-related variables dominate the water-quality signal captured by the index. The VIP radar representation in Figure 3b visualises the relative weighting structure across variables. Application of the TDS-based sensory penalty shifted the score distribution upward for the higher-scoring samples whilst leaving the overall ranking architecture largely intact, as shown by the sensitivity comparison in Figure S3. This indicates that the penalty is corrective in its effect on class severity at the upper end rather than structurally transformative of the broader quality ordering. Bayesian measurement-error propagation showed that the NPLS-GWQI classification was generally stable but not uniformly so. The median posterior classification confidence was 99.83%, but 11 of the 34 samples fell below 95% confidence and 3 fell below 60%, indicating that uncertainty was concentrated in a small subset of boundary cases rather than distributed across the dataset. The posterior most-probable class distribution remained dominated by the Excellent class (25 samples), but 2 samples classified deterministically as Very Poor were reassigned to Unsuitable as their posterior most-probable class, increasing the posterior Unsuitable count from 4 to 6 (Supplementary Table S2). The lowest posterior confidence was 46.0%, confirming that classification ambiguity is confined mainly to the degraded tail of the NPLS-GWQI distribution rather than to the large body of high-quality waters.

Benchmarking against conventional indexing alternatives shows that NPLS-GWQI preserves broad ordering while producing materially different class assignments in the degraded tail. The conventional arithmetic WQI (equal weights) and NPLS-GWQI were strongly rank-correlated (Table 22), but 8 of 34 samples differed in categorical class between the two methods, largely due to compression of poor/very-poor separation under the conventional index (Table 22). Under Monte Carlo weight perturbation for the conventional WQI, 5 samples fell below 95% class confidence (minimum 73.98%), indicating that weight uncertainty alone can introduce meaningful ambiguity around class boundaries (Table 23). The fuzzy-only baseline differs more strongly from NPLS-GWQI in class assignment and ranking in the moderate-to-poor transition region (Table 22), underscoring the decision relevance of explicitly modelling indeterminacy and falsity rather than truth membership alone.
