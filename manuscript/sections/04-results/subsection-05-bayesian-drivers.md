## 4.5 Bayesian Hydrochemical Driver Profiles and Pathway Synthesis

The Bayesian horseshoe regression results for the two endpoint models are summarised in Table 8. For NPLS-GWQI (Table 8a; Figure 6), EC was identified as the dominant driver, contributing 43.18% of the total normalised driver score with a positive directional effect, meaning that higher EC is associated with higher, that is, poorer, groundwater quality scores. The next most influential predictor was Na, with a 14.86% contribution and a negative effect, indicating that exchange-related sodium enrichment partly counterbalances the conductivity-driven rise in the quality index. K<sup>+</sup> contributed 7.77% positively, the silicate proxy contributed 5.27% positively, and TDS contributed 5.06% positively. The NPLS-GWQI endpoint is therefore controlled principally by mineralisation intensity, with a secondary suite of exchange and proxy terms partly opposing the dominant salinity signal.

For the fluoride endpoint (Table 8b; Figure 7), the leading drivers were $CAI_{2}$ at 23.16% with a negative directional effect, and EC at 22.96% with a positive effect. These terms are of comparable magnitude but opposing sign, indicating that fluoride concentration is governed by competing conductivity enrichment and ion-exchange suppression rather than a single monotonic pathway. The carbonate proxy contributed 9.22% negatively, and K<sup>+</sup> contributed 6.40% positively. The fluoride endpoint is therefore inherently multicausal.

Bayesian diagnostic and validation outputs are summarised in Table 19 and Figure S10. For both endpoints, posterior predictive checks reproduce the observed endpoint scale without systematic bias, and PSIS-LOO/WAIC summaries provide an explicit diagnostic audit of the regression layer. Prior sensitivity of ranked driver attributions to the global shrinkage scale is high for the dominant drivers (Table 20), indicating that the headline pathways (salinity control of NPLS-GWQI; competing exchange and mineralisation effects for fluoride) are not artefacts of a single τ setting. Nevertheless, the fluoride endpoint model remains less stable than the NPLS-GWQI model under both Bayesian diagnostics and sparse-PLS cross-validation (Table 9), and therefore fluoride driver attributions are interpreted more cautiously than NPLS-GWQI driver attributions.
