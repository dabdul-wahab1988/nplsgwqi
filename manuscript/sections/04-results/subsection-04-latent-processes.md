## 4.4 Latent Geochemical Signatures and Deterministic Constraints

Parallel analysis of the ILR-transformed compositional data retained five components, as summarised in Table 5 and Figure S1. PC1 through PC5 each exceeded the 95th-percentile random eigenvalue threshold, whilst PC6 and subsequent components fell below it, supporting a five-process representation as a data-driven dimensionality rather than an arbitrary truncation. Basis-sensitivity checks under alternative orthonormal ILR bases produced near-identical absolute CLR loading magnitudes (Table 21), indicating that the dominant process patterns are stable to SBP/basis perturbation.

The CLR loading profiles for the five retained processes, presented in Table 6 and Figure 4, resolve interpretable process families. The first process emphasises Na-Cl enrichment against depleted K. The second isolates a NO₃-Ca association against strongly negative SO₄. The third and fourth processes partition fluoride into Mg-F and K-F associations respectively, indicating that fluoride co-occurrence is governed by at least two geochemically distinguishable mechanisms. The fifth process couples Na and HCO₃ against negative Cl, marking an exchange-altered bicarbonate signature consistent with cation-exchange modification of original rock-weathering chemistry. Together, these loading structures show that mineralisation, ion exchange, and solute co-association operate simultaneously but remain distinguishable in compositional space.

The deterministic geochemical constraint diagnostics reported in Table 7 and Figure 5 corroborate these process interpretations. Thirty of the 34 samples occupy the rock-weathering field in the Gibbs framework, whilst 3 fall in the evaporation–concentration field and 1 in the precipitation-dominance field, establishing rock–water interaction as the primary geochemical regulator of the dataset with a minor evapoconcentration tail. The exchange indices show predominantly negative $CAI_{1}$ and $CAI_{2}$ values across the sample set, indicating widespread forward chloro-alkaline exchange in which sodium replaces calcium and magnesium from aquifer surfaces. Na/Cl molar ratios are generally elevated above unity, confirming that observed sodium concentrations cannot be explained by halite dissolution alone and that silicate weathering and exchange are substantial sodium sources. In combination, these diagnostics support a system dominated by rock–water interaction and subsequently modified by ion exchange during hydrochemical evolution. Bootstrap stability assessment of the absolute CLR loading structure, shown in Figure S4, confirmed that the five-process architecture is reproducible at the level of dominant loading magnitudes across resamples, though some secondary ion-process pairs showed greater variability than others.

Saturation-index evaluation further supports the carbonate-buffering and fluoride-mobilization interpretation. At 25 °C, fluorite SI values are negative for all samples (median SI(F) = −2.64; max = −0.58), indicating undersaturation and therefore no thermodynamic constraint requiring fluorite precipitation under the observed compositions (Table 18; Figure S9). Calcite and dolomite are predominantly undersaturated but include a small number of near-saturated to slightly supersaturated cases (Table 18), consistent with heterogeneous carbonate buffering across the borehole network.
