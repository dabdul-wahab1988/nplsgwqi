## 4.7 Irrigation Suitability

Entropy-derived weights and the N-ISI class distribution are reported in Table 12 and Table 13. PI, PS, and KR received the greatest entropy-assigned weights, confirming that these high-dispersion, high-leverage indices dominate the composite suitability score. The seven indices, SAR, SSP, MH, KR, PI, RSC, and PS, show strong distributional dispersion across the 34 samples, with particularly large spread and prominent outliers in KR and PI (Figure 10c), indicating that a subset of samples carries disproportionately poor irrigation characteristics relative to the broader background of moderate conditions (Figure 10c).

Per-sample irrigation indices and their neutrosophic and classical categorisations are reported in Table 24. When N-ISI classes are compared with single-criterion classical decision categories (SAR-, SSP-, and RSC-based), disagreement is substantial (Table 25), reflecting that N-ISI is an entropy-weighted aggregation over seven indices while classical criteria are univariate screens. The comparison therefore highlights where an aggregated multi-index suitability judgement would diverge from a single-index decision rule, which is particularly relevant for marginal cases where salinity and sodicity indicators are not concordant.

The final N-ISI classification (Table 13; Figure 10d) is dominated by the marginal class, which encompassed 23 samples representing 67.65% of the dataset. Five samples were classified as excellent, 3 as good, and 3 as unsuitable. The scatter panels in Figure 10a and 10b, show that poorer N-ISI outcomes are concentrated in the more saline and sodic portions of the EC-SSP and EC-SAR space.

Under the same Bayesian measurement-error propagation, the N-ISI classification proved markedly more stable than the NPLS-GWQI classification. The posterior most-probable class distribution reproduced the deterministic class totals exactly (23 Marginal, 5 Excellent, 3 Good, and 3 Unsuitable), the median posterior confidence was 100%, and only 2 of the 34 samples fell below 95% confidence (Supplementary Table S3). Those 2 cases remained within the Good class as their posterior most-probable assignment, with only minor posterior spillover toward Marginal; the minimum posterior confidence was still 89.17%. These results indicate that the irrigation suitability classes are sharply separated in the present dataset and materially less sensitive to plausible analytical uncertainty than the boundary cases observed in the NPLS-GWQI classification.
