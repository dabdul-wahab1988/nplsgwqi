## 5.1 Hydrochemical Evolution in the Mapped Voltaian Setting

The dominance of rock-weathering control, established by the Gibbs-domain assignment of 30 of 34 samples to the rock-weathering field (Table 7; Figure 6), is consistent with the broader literature on sedimentary groundwater systems in northern Ghana and the Voltaian basin. Silicate and carbonate weathering, alongside reverse ion exchange, have similarly been identified as the principal controls on groundwater chemistry in the Talensi District <sup>4</sup>, and fluoride enrichment in Saboba District has been linked to hydrogeochemical processes arising from rock-water interaction rather than to exogenous contamination <sup>6</sup>. The present findings extend this geogenic weathering narrative into Karaga District, where the Bimbila Formation’s wide areal extent ensures that the majority of sampled waters evolve principally through dissolution of siliciclastic and associated carbonate minerals.

The five latent processes resolved by ILR-PCA, Na-Cl enrichment, NO₃-Ca association, Mg-F association, K-F association, and Na-HCO₃ exchange, map coherently onto the geological heterogeneity of the study area. The Na-HCO₃ exchange signature, expressed as the fifth latent process with positive Na and HCO₃ loadings against negative Cl, is consistent with the widely negative $CAI_{1}$ and $CAI_{2}$ values that indicate forward cation exchange in which sodium displaces calcium and magnesium from aquifer mineral surfaces. This exchange mechanism accounts for the observed sodium enrichment beyond what halite dissolution alone could explain, as confirmed by the elevated Na/Cl molar ratios documented in Table 7. Comparable exchange signatures have been reported across Voltaian and crystalline basement aquifer systems in northern Ghana and adjacent settings <sup>5,26,34</sup>.

The two fluoride-partitioning processes, Mg-F and K-F, suggest that fluoride in Karaga District groundwater is not associated with a single mineral source or a single geochemical pathway. Fluoride co-occurrence with magnesium may reflect dissolution of fluorite or fluoride-bearing silicates in the presence of magnesium-releasing minerals, whilst the K-F association may relate to potassium feldspar weathering releasing both ions simultaneously. The competing negative effect of $CAI_{2}$ and positive effect of EC in the Bayesian fluoride model (Table 8; Figure 7) reinforces the multicausal character of fluoride enrichment, showing that conductivity-driven concentration and ion-exchange suppression interact to determine the final fluoride level at any given borehole. This complexity makes Karaga District groundwater fluoride behaviour consistent with the broader national pattern described by Araya et al. (2022) and Sunkari <sup>5,26</sup>, where fluoride hazard reflects a convergence of geochemical conditions rather than a simple monotonic function of any single control.

The saturation-index analysis (Table 18; Figure S9) provides additional mechanistic support for this interpretation. Fluorite is undersaturated across the sampled compositions under the evaluated temperature band, indicating that observed fluoride levels are not constrained by fluorite precipitation equilibrium, and are therefore consistent with fluoride persistence under rock–water interaction and evapoconcentration pathways. Predominantly undersaturated calcite/dolomite with a small number of near-saturated cases suggests spatially heterogeneous carbonate buffering, which can modulate calcium activity and thereby influence the fluoride system indirectly. In this context, “conductivity enrichment” is interpreted explicitly as evapoconcentration and salinity increase along the hydrochemical evolution pathway rather than as a distinct process family.
