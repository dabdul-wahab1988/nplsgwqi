## 5.2 Groundwater-Quality Structure

The strong skew of the NPLS-GWQI distribution, 25 of 34 samples classified as excellent, with only 8 samples in degraded categories (poor, very poor, or unsuitable), reflects the geogenic weathering template of the Bimbila Formation, where most boreholes tap waters that have not undergone sufficient mineralisation to exceed multiple guideline thresholds simultaneously. The dominant weighting of EC in the bootstrapped N-VIP scheme (Table 4) confirms that the groundwater quality score is primarily an expression of mineralisation intensity rather than a multi-parameter deficit distributed evenly across all variables. This finding aligns with studies in northern Ghana that document broadly acceptable groundwater chemistry at most sampled sites, punctuated by a smaller subset of highly mineralised or fluoride-enriched waters with significantly degraded quality indicators <sup>7,34</sup>.

The four samples classified as unsuitable are not ambiguous or borderline cases, as indicated by their low indeterminacy values in Table 3. This clarity of separation at the degraded end of the distribution is a particular strength of the neutrosophic framework relative to conventional hard-boundary arithmetic indices, which can misrepresent the confidence of class assignments near threshold values <sup>9,10</sup>. The bootstrapped VIP weighting further addresses the sensitivity of conventional fixed-weight indices to arbitrary design choices, producing a data-driven weight structure that reflects the actual explanatory burden of each variable within the dataset rather than an assumed contribution derived from expert opinion or literature precedent. The insensitivity of the overall quality ranking to the sensory penalty inclusion or exclusion, demonstrated by Figure S2, also indicates that the principal quality gradient in Karaga District groundwater is driven by dissolved-ion chemistry.
