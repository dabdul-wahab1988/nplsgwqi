## 5.3 Interpreting the Bayesian Driver Profiles

The concentration of NPLS-GWQI driver influence on EC at 43.18% (Table 8a; Figure 6) provides a clear mechanistic link between the hydrochemical evolution identified in process space and the water-quality outcome measured in assessment space. EC integrates the total ionic mineralisation of the groundwater sample and is therefore a composite signal of the cumulative product of rock weathering, ion exchange, and any evaporative concentration that has occurred along the flow path. That a single conductivity variable captures almost half of the total driver budget confirms that the Karaga District groundwater quality gradient is fundamentally a mineralisation gradient, with the degraded waters representing the extreme tail of a geochemically evolved population rather than discretely contaminated outliers.

The suppressing role of Na in the NPLS-GWQI model, contributing 14.86% in a negative direction (Table 8a; Figure 6), is initially counterintuitive, given that Na is itself a mineralisation marker. This pattern is best understood as a consequence of the ion-exchange signature documented in the latent processes and geochemical constraints: where sodium is elevated through exchange rather than simple dissolution, its independent effect on the quality index may be partly captured by EC whilst its residual coefficient reflects the exchange substitution of less impactful ions. The Sankey pathway in Figure 6 makes this balance visible, showing that the NPLS-GWQI endpoint is the net result of a dominant mineralisation pressure moderated by a secondary suite of exchange-related and proxy-based opposing controls.

For fluoride, the near-equal opposing contributions of $CAI_{2}$ (23.16% negative) and EC (22.96% positive) reveal a system in which two coherent geochemical forces, ion-exchange context and overall mineralisation, are competing to set the final fluoride level (Table 8b; Figure 7). This balance explains why simple models relating fluoride exclusively to conductivity or exclusively to exchange indices fit the fluoride endpoint less cleanly than the NPLS-GWQI, as reflected in the weaker R² and cross-validation performance of the fluoride models reported in Table 9. The model diagnostics therefore reinforce that fluoride in this setting should not be treated as a straightforward mineralisation by-product but as the outcome of multiple interacting geochemical controls whose balance varies across the sampling network.
