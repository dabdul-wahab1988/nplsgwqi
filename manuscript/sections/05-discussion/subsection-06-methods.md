## 5.6 Methodological Strengths, Limitations, and Transferability

The principal methodological contribution of this study is the implementation of a dual-space framework that formally separates compositional process discovery from uncertainty-aware assessment modelling. The ILR transformation applied before PCA addresses the closure problem inherent in groundwater major-ion data, avoiding the spurious correlations and distorted loadings that can emerge when raw-space multivariate methods are applied to compositional hydrochemical datasets <sup>12,13</sup>. Parallel analysis provides a theoretically grounded component retention rule rather than relying on visual scree-plot inspection, and bootstrap stability assessment of the CLR loadings provides a resampling-based quantification of loading robustness that is rarely included in district-scale hydrochemical studies. The neutrosophic architecture for both NPLS-GWQI and N-ISI explicitly encodes classification uncertainty through truth-indeterminacy-falsity membership, and the Bayesian horseshoe regression framework in the driver models provides posterior uncertainty quantification and formal feature sparsity, addressing well-documented limitations of both deterministic WQI design and fixed-weight driver attribution <sup>9,10,38</sup>. Also, the posterior class-probability analysis was able to shows that analytical uncertainty exerts a stronger effect on degraded drinking-water class assignments than on irrigation suitability classes in the present dataset: NPLS-GWQI uncertainty is concentrated in a small number of boundary samples, whereas the N-ISI classification remains almost unchanged under the same 5% analytical-error propagation.

The principal limitations of the present study are threefold. First, the sample size of 34 is small relative to the dimensionality of the augmented predictor block, and although robust PCA, horseshoe shrinkage, and bootstrap resampling were applied to mitigate this, the statistical power for fine-grained sub-group analyses and spatial generalisation is limited. Comparison with modelling studies of similar scale in northern Ghana suggests that the core hydrochemical findings are robust, but any extrapolation to unsampled parts of the district should be treated with caution. Second, the dataset represents a single sampling campaign and does not capture temporal variability across the seasonal cycle. In a semi-arid district with pronounced dry-season concentration effects, a single-season snapshot may underestimate the range of groundwater quality conditions that residents encounter across the year. Third, the absence of trace metals, iron, lead, arsenic, cadmium, and nickel, from the dataset limits the scope of the health-risk assessment to fluoride and nitrate and precludes evaluation of trace-metal-related exposure pathways that may be relevant in some parts of the district.
