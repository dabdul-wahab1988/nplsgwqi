## 5.5 Irrigation and Groundwater-Use Implications

The predominance of the marginal N-ISI class, covering 23 of 34 samples or 67.65% of the dataset, indicates that Karaga District groundwater is not uniformly unsuitable for irrigation but presents a broad middle band of chemically constrained waters that require management caution rather than blanket prohibition. Marginal irrigation suitability in the present context reflects primarily the influence of the high-leverage PI, PS, and KR indices (Table 12), which capture permeability reduction risk, potential salinity effects, and sodium-to-calcium and magnesium balance. These constraints are consistent with the rock-weathering-dominated, bicarbonate-enriched, and selectively sodic character of the groundwater documented in the hydrochemical analysis, and they suggest that the principal irrigation concerns in Karaga District are sodicity and permeability hazard rather than simple total salinity.

The existence of 5 excellent and 3 good irrigation-quality samples within the dataset confirms that acceptable irrigation water is available within the district, and the spatial distribution of these samples relative to the geological units, as shown by the N-ISI class overlay in Figure 10, provides an initial basis for site-specific water-use planning. The 3 unsuitable samples, concentrated in the more saline and sodic portion of the EC-SSP and EC-SAR space (Figure 10a and 10b), should be excluded from irrigation without dilution or treatment. This mixed suitability profile mirrors findings from comparable northern Ghanaian settings, where groundwater has been found to support some irrigation uses whilst failing others depending on the parameter and threshold applied <sup>2,34</sup>. The entropy weighting applied through N-ISI provides a more defensible basis for composite suitability scoring than equal-weight or subjectively assigned approaches, because it ensures that the indices with greatest dispersion, and therefore greatest discriminating power, contribute proportionally more to the final classification <sup>38</sup>.
